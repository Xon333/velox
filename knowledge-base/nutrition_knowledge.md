# NUTRITION KNOWLEDGE — ADVANCED PRINCIPLES

> **Scope:** This file covers advanced nutrition topics not in cycling_database.md. Basic in-ride targets, recovery nutrition window, race weight, daily macro baseline, hydration overview, and common mistakes are defined there. This file does not repeat them.

---

## 1. FUEL FOR THE WORK REQUIRED (FFTWR)

### Core Principle
Protein and fat stay roughly stable day to day. Carbohydrate moves with training demand. The goal is to fuel the session, not just the day — specifically the 12–18 hours before a key session, not only the day itself.

Energy availability has two thresholds to know:
- **30 kcal/kg LBM/day** — floor below which hormonal function, recovery, and bone density begin to degrade
- **45 kcal/kg LBM/day** — sustainability floor for performance on training days; staying below this during build blocks causes adaptation to stall

---

### Daily Carbohydrate by Day Type (75kg example)

| Day Type | Carbs/kg | Total (75kg) | Practical Approach |
|----------|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Rest day | 2–3 g/kg | 150–225g | Meals lean protein-and-veg heavy; smaller starchy portions |
| Moderate training (90-min endurance) | 5–6 g/kg | 375–450g | Normal portions with rice/pasta; replenish post-ride |
| Hard training (intervals) | 7–8 g/kg | 525–600g | Front-load: bigger starchy portions at every meal, extra fruit, sports drink during |
| Competition / big sportive | 8–12 g/kg | 600–900g | See Race-Day Timeline section |
| Recovery week | 3–5 g/kg | 225–375g | Matches reduced demand; don't undereat protein |

The night before a hard session matters as much as the morning of. A low-carb dinner before a Thursday interval session is self-sabotage.

---

### Big Blocks
Consecutive hard training days (e.g. intervals Wednesday, tempo Thursday, long ride Saturday) draw down the same glycogen stores in sequence. Each session starts partially depleted if carbs between sessions match a rest-day intake. Big Blocks get big fuel — high carbs on the evenings between them, not just on the hard days themselves.

---

### Train-Low: When and How
Training with low glycogen availability drives fat-oxidation and mitochondrial adaptation signals. It is a precision tool, not a lifestyle.

**Correct use:** A single easy Z2 session per week, ridden fasted or after a carb-light previous evening. Intensity must stay genuinely easy (< 65% max HR, no over-threshold pushes). Restore carbohydrates at the next meal.

**Never:** Key sessions (threshold, VO2max, any interval work). Train-low does not mean chronically under-fuelling and calling it a strategy.

## 2. NUTRITION PERIODISATION BY TRAINING PHASE

### Base Phase (10–12 hrs/week, mostly Z2)

| Macronutrient | Daily Target | Notes |
|--------------|-------------|-------|
| Carbohydrate | 4–6 g/kg | Lower end on rest days; upper end on long-ride days |
| Protein | 1.6–1.8 g/kg | Upper end protects lean mass if in a moderate caloric squeeze |
| Fat | Fill remaining energy | Olive oil, oily fish, nuts; no primary manipulation needed |

Base phase is the correct window for deliberate body-composition work if needed — small daily deficit (200–400 kcal), never on hard training days, strength training maintained to protect lean mass.

---

### Build Phase (12–15 hrs/week, two hard sessions)

| Macronutrient | Daily Target | Notes |
|--------------|-------------|-------|
| Carbohydrate | 6–8 g/kg | Push to upper end on hard-session days; 90–120g/hr in-ride is non-negotiable |
| Protein | 1.8–2.0 g/kg | Strength work + interval muscle damage raises turnover floor |
| Fat | Fill remaining energy | Do not get clever with fat restriction here |

**Most common amateur mistake:** Keeping base-phase food intake during a build block. When training volume and intensity rise but calories don't follow, energy availability drops 8–10 kcal/kg/day below the adaptation threshold. The rider hits week 7–8 feeling flat and blames fitness. The cause is under-fuelling.

**Never attempt weight loss during a build block.** The body cannot adapt to higher training loads in caloric deficit. Body-composition work belongs in the base or off-season, not in the 8 weeks producing the watts.

---

### Race Week (Final 7 Days)

| Phase | Carbohydrate | Notes |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| Days 1–4 | 6–8 g/kg | Continue build-phase intake; maintain training quality |
| Days 5–7 (final 48–72 hrs) | 8–10 g/kg | Glycogen super-compensation; low-fibre sources |

**Correct sources for final 48–72 hrs:** White rice, white bread, white pasta, low-fibre cereal, fruit, simple sugars. High-fibre sources at this volume cause GI distress; the goal is glycogen storage, not bowel health.

**Scale weight will rise 1–2 kg by race morning.** Glycogen binds 3–4g water per gram stored. A rider 1.5 kg heavier than their build-block average on the start line has full glycogen and the water that proves it. This is correct. Do not panic-restrict the night before the event.

## 3. PROTEIN: TIMING, DISTRIBUTION, AND MASTERS CONSIDERATIONS

### The Distribution Problem
Most amateur cyclists under-deliver on protein because of a weak morning pattern, not a weak overall awareness. A typical day might look like:
- Breakfast: coffee + toast = 5–10g protein
- Lunch: 25g
- Dinner: 35–45g
- **Total: 70–80g → 0.93–1.07g/kg for a 75kg rider (below the 1.6g/kg floor)**

The morning gap is the single most correctable protein error.

---

### Practical Distribution (75kg target: 120–150g/day)

| Meal/Timing | Target Protein | Easy Sources |
|-------------|---------------|--------------|
| Breakfast | 20–30g | Greek yoghurt (15–20g) + eggs; or protein smoothie |
| Mid-morning snack (if breakfast is light) | 15–20g | Cottage cheese, Greek yoghurt, protein bar |
| Post-ride feed | 25–35g | Recovery shake, chocolate milk, chicken + rice |
| Lunch | 25–35g | Chicken, fish, eggs, legumes |
| Dinner | 30–40g | Meat, fish, tofu + grains |
| Pre-bed | 20–30g | Casein-rich: cottage cheese, Greek yoghurt (slow-digesting; maintains amino acid availability overnight) |

Four to five protein servings across the day, including the post-ride feed and the pre-bed feed, covers most riders.

---

### Masters Cyclists (40+): Higher Threshold
The protein-leucine threshold required to trigger muscle protein synthesis rises with age. Older athletes need both:
- **Higher absolute intake:** 1.8–2.2g/kg/day (not 1.6g/kg)
- **More even distribution:** No meal below ~30g protein; the 22-year-old can skip breakfast, the 48-year-old cannot

The pre-bed casein feed is especially important for masters athletes — overnight muscle-protein synthesis is a meaningful adaptation lever that is frequently left on the table.

## 4. CAFFEINE PROTOCOL

### Performance Effect
Caffeine improves endurance performance by 2–4% across pooled studies. In a 40km TT, that equates to roughly 1–2 minutes. Mechanisms:
- Blocks adenosine receptors (reduces perceived effort — the dominant factor)
- Increases fat oxidation (spares early glycogen)
- Improves neuromuscular recruitment
- Enhances focus during long efforts

---

### Dosing

| Body Weight | Low Dose (3mg/kg) | High Dose (6mg/kg) |
|------------|------------------|------------------|
| 60kg | 180mg | 360mg |
| 70kg | 210mg | 420mg |
| 75kg | 225mg | 450mg |
| 80kg | 240mg | 480mg |
| 90kg | 270mg | 540mg |

**Reference amounts:** Strong espresso ~75mg; caffeine gel ~30–50mg; cola can ~35mg; caffeine tablet (precise, cheap, no calories — the most controlled option).

---

### Timing

| Situation | Protocol |
|-----------|---------|
| Pre-race / pre-hard session | 3–6mg/kg, 45–60 min before start (peak blood concentration at ~45–60 min) |
| Mid-ride top-up (events > 3 hrs) | 1–2mg/kg at midpoint; maintains caffeine levels |
| Late-ride fatigue (final 60–90 min) | 1–2mg/kg via gel, flat cola, or caffeinated drink |

---

### Tolerance and Sleep

**Regular users:** Tolerance reduces but does not eliminate the ergogenic effect. Two options:
1. **Withdrawal 2–3 days before event** to restore sensitivity — downsides: headaches, irritability, possibly disrupted training
2. **Maintain and dose at higher end (5–6mg/kg)** — most coaches favour this; withdrawal downsides rarely justify the marginal gain

**Half-life is 5–6 hours.** A 300mg dose at 2pm leaves 150mg in the system at 8pm. For afternoon events, use the lower dose range. For morning events, dose freely.

**Genetic metabolisers vary.** Some riders experience anxiety or GI symptoms above 3–4mg/kg. Always test the exact protocol in training before race day. Never trial a new caffeine dose on an event day.

## 5. RACE-DAY 24-HOUR TIMELINE

### T−24 hours (Day Before)
- Carbohydrate at 8–10 g/kg from low-fibre sources (white rice, white pasta, cereal, fruit)
- Drink steadily through the day — glycogen storage binds 3–4g water per gram; front-load fluid into the first 12 waking hours
- Dinner 3–4 hrs before bed: familiar 100–150g carbohydrate meal from foods rehearsed before hard training rides (no new foods, no unknown restaurants)
- No alcohol — suppresses REM sleep, reduces overnight HRV, causes slight dehydration; reliably costs 3–5% of next-day power
- Small casein-rich protein before bed (Greek yoghurt, cottage cheese) for overnight muscle recovery

### T−12 hours (Sleep)
Sleep is part of fuelling. Aim for 8 hours in bed. The two nights before the event matter more than the night before — good sleep three nights out partially compensates for race-night anxiety.

### T−4 to T−3.5 hours (Pre-Race Meal)
**Target:** 1.5–2g carbohydrate/kg body weight from familiar, easily-digested food (must be rehearsed at least 3 times before long training rides).

| Option | Carbohydrate |
|--------|-------------|
| Porridge (70g oats) + banana + honey | ~100g |
| 2 slices white toast + jam + bowl low-fibre cereal + skim milk | ~110g |
| Rice pudding (200g) + banana + fruit smoothie | ~115g |

A small protein component (egg, scoop of whey, nut butter) slows glucose response and reduces reactive blood-sugar drop in warm-up. Keep fat and fibre low — slows gastric emptying.

### T−60 to T−90 minutes (Caffeine + Top-Up)
- Caffeine at 3–6mg/kg, 45–60 min before start
- Small carbohydrate top-up: 30–40g (banana, single energy bar, half a bottle of sports drink)
- No solid food from this point until the start

### T−20 minutes (Warm-Up)
- Sip 200–300ml water + electrolytes during warm-up
- Optional: one gel in the final 10 min of warm-up (carbohydrate in bloodstream by the gun)
- Flat/sportive start: 15–20 min progressive warm-up; hilly start with early climbs: 25–35 min with 2–3 brief threshold-or-above pushes

### T+0 (Start)
Begin at controlled pace. Adrenaline drives most riders 5–8% over sustainable pace in the first 15 minutes. Start eating at 20 minutes — not when hungry, not when flagging; from 20 minutes.

### T+0 to Finish (In-Event Fuelling)
**Target:** 90–120g carbohydrate/hr from the first hour, using a 2:1 glucose:fructose mix.

**Why 2:1 glucose:fructose:** Glucose absorbs through the SGLT1 intestinal transporter, which saturates at ~60g/hr. Fructose uses a separate transporter (GLUT5). Combining the two doubles the absorption ceiling. Pure glucose or pure maltodextrin products cap delivery at ~60g/hr — insufficient for events over 3 hrs.

**Fuelling cadence:** 1 gel every 25–30 min, or 1 bottle of carbohydrate drink every 30–45 min. Set a recurring 20-min alarm on the bike computer.

**Hydration:** 500–750ml/hr; 500–800mg sodium/litre. Calibrate to conditions and individual sweat rate.

**Hour 4+:** Switch to savoury or real-food sources — rice cakes with salt, boiled potatoes, small sandwich, salted nuts. Flavour change reduces gel fatigue and GI distress. Carbohydrate type matters less than continuing to consume at the target rate.

**Bonk-proofing rule:** Arriving at hour 4 with an empty bottle and < 40g carbs consumed in the previous hour means the hole is too deep to recover from. Fuelling must stay ahead of demand throughout.

### T+0 to T+60 (Post-Finish)
**Within 60 minutes:** 1.0–1.2g/kg carbohydrate + 20–30g protein. Glycogen storage rates are highest immediately post-exercise and begin declining thereafter.

Liquid form is fine and often easier: recovery shake, protein + carb smoothie, or chocolate milk (convenient 4:1 carb:protein ratio).

Within 2 hours: proper balanced meal. Hold protein at 1.6–2.0g/kg through the following 48 hours. Prioritise sleep — an extra hour in bed for the next two nights is the cheapest recovery intervention available post-event.

## 6. HYDRATION: FULL PROTOCOL

### Fluid Targets by Conditions

| Conditions | Fluid per Hour |
|-----------|--------------|
| Cool (< 15°C) | 400–500ml |
| Moderate (15–25°C) | 500–700ml |
| Hot (> 25°C) | 700–900ml |
| Extreme heat (> 35°C) | 900ml+ |

Individual sweat rates vary enormously (500ml–1.5L/hr). **Personal calibration:** Weigh before and after a 1-hour ride with no drinking and no bathroom break. Every kg lost ≈ 1 litre sweat.

A 2% body weight drop through fluid loss reliably raises heart rate 5–10 bpm at the same power output and begins degrading performance. Thirst signals dehydration that is already 1–2% underway — do not wait for thirst.

---

### Sodium

**Target:** 500–700mg sodium/hr on rides > 2 hours or in hot conditions. Sweat sodium concentration varies (500–1500mg/L); heavy or salty sweaters need the upper end.

**Two-bottle system:** Electrolytes in one bottle, energy drink or plain water in the other — allows sodium and carbohydrate intake to be adjusted independently.

---

### Pre-Ride Hydration
500ml in the hour before rolling out. This is part of the fuelling protocol, not optional.

---

### Signs of Under and Over-Hydration

| Under-Hydrating | Over-Hydrating (Hyponatraemia) |
|----------------|-------------------------------|
| HR creeping up at same power | Nausea and bloating |
| Headache mid-ride | Fluid intake far exceeding sweat rate |
| Dark urine post-ride | Plain water only on very long rides (no sodium) |
| Foggy or irritable | Swelling in hands or feet |
| Cramps in final third of long ride | |

Hyponatraemia (over-drinking diluting blood sodium) is more common in ultra-endurance amateur events than dehydration. Match fluid to need.

---

### Indoor Training
Sweat rate indoors is 30–50% higher than outdoors at the same effort due to lack of convective cooling. Target 750ml+ per hour indoors regardless of ambient temperature.
