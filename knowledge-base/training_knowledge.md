# TRAINING KNOWLEDGE — ADVANCED PRINCIPLES

> **Scope:** This file covers advanced training topics not in cycling_database.md. Zone 2, polarised 80/20, FTP basics, periodisation, and weekly structure are defined there. This file does not repeat them.

---

## 1. POWER METER USAGE

### Four-Level Framework

| Level | What It Means | Where Most Amateurs Are |
|-------|--------------|------------------------|
| 1 — Zone definition | Set zones from a current FTP test; train within zones | Most amateurs operate here |
| 2 — Session discipline | Use zones to execute sessions cleanly; easy stays easy, threshold hits Z4 | Some level 2 execution |
| 3 — Pacing discipline | Use power to pace climbs, time trials, and long efforts | Rarely reached |
| 4 — Diagnostic tracking | Track slower-moving metrics across weeks; identify limiters | Usually requires coaching support |

The number on the screen is not the point. The behaviour it drives is the point.

---

### Key Metrics Beyond FTP

FTP alone gives an incomplete picture. Track these three alongside it:

| Metric | What It Measures | How to Test | What a Trend Tells You |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **5-minute power** | VO2max proxy; predicts short climb and attack performance | Max sustainable 5-min effort; once per block | Rising 5-min power with stable FTP = VO2max developing even when FTP looks flat |
| **Durability** | Power retention at the end of long rides | Compare matched intervals at hour 1 vs hour 4 of a 4+ hour ride | >10% drop = base underdevelopment; trained target is 3–5% |
| **Short-effort repeat power** | Attacking and counter-attacking capacity | 8×30 sec / 30 sec rest; record all 8 efforts | Track average and decline from rep 1 to rep 8 |

Trained cyclists hold 3–5% drop-off across a 4-hour ride; most amateurs drop 10–15%. Durability improvements move race performance more than raising peak power, because races are decided in the late stages.

---

### Power vs Heart Rate — When Each Is the Better Tool

| Situation | Better Tool | Why |
|-----------|------------|-----|
| Short precise intervals (< 5 min) | **Power** | HR lags 1–2 min; too slow to be useful for a 4-min VO2max effort |
| Easy / recovery rides | **Heart rate** | Power can mislead — a strong cyclist produces 200W at Z2; HR gives the true ceiling |
| Long rides (> 2 hrs) | **Both** | Power confirms pace; HR identifies aerobic decoupling (HR climbing while power stays steady = fatigue accumulating) |
| Group rides, races, terrain-driven efforts | **RPE in-ride, Power post-ride** | Power is the post-ride analysis tool; RPE is the real-time pacing input |
| Indoor structured ERG workouts | **Power** | ERG holds the watts; RPE is the post-session calibration check |

**The signal in divergence:** When power and HR disagree, that divergence is information.
- Same watts, HR 15+ bpm higher than usual → fatigue or under-recovery
- Same HR, watts 5–10% higher than usual → fitness gained; consider recalibrating zones

---

### TT and Long-Effort Pacing Principles

- **Hold the prescribed watts; trust the math.** The first 5 minutes of a TT always feel too easy for the prescribed power — that is normal. Going harder than prescribed in those first 5 minutes is the most common amateur TT mistake.
- **Climbs:** Steady power through the climb even if it means dropping back; accelerate where gradient allows efficient power deployment. Most amateurs surge on climbs and recover on descents — the opposite of optimal pacing.
- **Final 5 minutes:** This is where you spend what's left. Most riders finish with capacity unused. Correct TT pacing has the cyclist near their limit for the final 5 minutes, holding form and power.
- **Lumpy courses:** Slightly above threshold on climbs, slightly below in valleys. Flat courses: steady threshold throughout.

---

### Review Cadence

| Timeframe | What to Review |
|-----------|---------------|
| Weekly | Total training hours; key session execution (did intensity targets land?); resting HR trend |
| Monthly | Rolling 7-day HRV direction; body composition direction; sleep quality trend; peak power numbers (5-min, 1-min, 5-sec) |
| Every 6–8 weeks | FTP test on consistent protocol; compare to previous test |
| Seasonally | Review full block against periodisation plan; identify what worked and what didn't; adjust next block |

Daily reactions to power numbers add noise without signal. Multi-week trends are what matter.

---

### Common Power Data Mistakes

- **Zone obsession** — modifying pace mid-session to stay exactly inside a zone rather than executing the session. Zones are guidelines; the session is the work.
- **Testing too often** — every 4 weeks disrupts training. Every 6–8 weeks.
- **Ignoring RPE** — power meters can have calibration drift. If RPE strongly disagrees with power, RPE is the sanity check.
- **Chasing the FTP number** — training for the FTP test produces good test results, not fitness. Train the underlying systems; FTP follows.

## 2. LACTATE THRESHOLD TESTING

### Why FTP Isn't Enough

FTP is a single capacity number — approximately sustainable 1-hour power. The standard zones (Z2 = 56–75% FTP) work well for cyclists whose LT1 happens to sit in that range. Two riders with identical FTPs can have very different LT1s depending on training history:
- Deep aerobic base → LT1 may sit at ~70% FTP
- FTP built through threshold-heavy training without base → LT1 may sit at ~55% FTP

If LT1 is 15W lower than assumed, every "easy" ride is drifting into early tempo. Multiplied across a year of training, this is a major hidden leak.

**LT1 (aerobic threshold):** ~2 mmol/L blood lactate. The point where lactate production starts to outpace baseline clearance. Below this: deep aerobic adaptation. Above it: carbohydrate use increases, adaptation profile shifts.

**LT2 (lactate threshold / MLSS):** ~4 mmol/L blood lactate. Production accelerates beyond what clearance can handle. Physiologically equivalent to FTP but anchored to a measurable event rather than a time-trial result.

The zone between LT1 and LT2 is the grey zone — too hard for deep aerobic adaptation, not hard enough for high-intensity adaptation.

---

### Home Test Kit (~$250 setup, ~$25/test in consumables)

| Item | Cost (approx.) |
|------|---------------|
| Lactate meter (Lactate Plus or equivalent) | ~$250 |
| Test strips (box of 25) | ~$80–120 |
| Lancets + alcohol wipes | ~$15–20 |

One box of strips lasts 3–4 tests at 6–8 strips per test. Pick one meter and stick with it — repeatability matters more than absolute accuracy.

---

### Step Test Protocol (5-Minute Steps)

**Equipment:** Smart trainer or flat road; lancets, strips, alcohol wipes, meter, notebook.

**Warm-up:** 15–20 min easy; 2–3 short pickups to wake legs.

**Starting power:** ~50% of FTP.

**Each step:**
1. Hold power for 5 minutes
2. In the last 30 seconds: draw blood from the side of a fingertip (not the pad)
3. Use the second drop, not the first (first contains tissue fluid)
4. Wipe finger with alcohol wipe, let dry before lancing
5. Record: lactate value, average power, average heart rate

**Step size:** Increase by 20–25W each step.

**End the test:** When lactate is clearly above 4 mmol/L and rising fast (~5–6 mmol/L is enough). Total test time: 45–60 min including warm-up.

---

### Reading Results

Plot lactate (y-axis) vs power (x-axis). The curve will:
1. Start almost flat → LT1 is where it first bends upward (~2 mmol/L)
2. Bend more steeply → LT2 is the second inflection point (~4 mmol/L)

**Typical masters cyclist results:**
- LT1: 60–75% of FTP (varies by aerobic depth)
- LT2: 90–100% of FTP

If LT1 is 10–20W below the top of the FTP-percentage-based Zone 2, every "easy" ride has been drifting into early tempo. This is the most common finding and the most correctable one.

---

### Using LT1/LT2 Day to Day

| Session Type | Power Target |
|-------------|-------------|
| Easy / Z2 rides | Below LT1 |
| Sweet spot | Between LT1 and LT2 (use in moderation) |
| Threshold intervals | At or just below LT2 |
| VO2max intervals | 105–115% of LT2 power |

If standard FTP-percentage zones and lactate test results disagree, trust the lactate test.

---

### When to Test

| When | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Start of base season (Oct–Nov) | Set zones; begin training below LT1 |
| End of base block (Jan) | Confirm LT1 has risen; curve should shift right |
| Mid-build (Mar–Apr) | Confirm LT2 is rising before key event |
| Race season | No testing — trust the work |

Most masters cyclists: 2–3 tests per year. LT1 moves seasonally (rises after base block, drops after illness). Training off out-of-date numbers is one of the most common quiet causes of underperformance.

## 3. RPE / POWER TWO-CHANNEL SYSTEM

Power measures the work. RPE measures the cost. The pair is the system — neither alone is complete.

### Borg CR-10 Scale for Cyclists

| RPE | Description | Zone Equivalent |
|-----|------------|----------------|
| 0 | Rest | — |
| 1–2 | Very easy; recovery spin; could hold a phone call | Z1 |
| 3–4 | Easy / endurance; full conversation; legs not stressed | Z2 |
| 5–6 | Moderate; short sentences; "comfortable hard"; could hold for an hour | Z3 / sweet spot |
| 7 | Threshold; sentences become harder; heavy rhythmic breathing | Z4 |
| 8 | VO2 / very hard; single-word answers; 4–8 min maximum | Z5 |
| 9 | Near-maximal; cannot speak; 1–2 min maximum | Z6 |
| 10 | Sprint; seconds; nothing in reserve | Z7 |

A rider internalises this scale within 6–8 weeks of structured training.

---

### The Four Divergences Worth Catching

| Divergence | What It Looks Like | What It Means | Action |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|--------|
| **Power on target, RPE creeping up** | 250W sweet spot felt like RPE 6 four weeks ago; now RPE 7–8 | Fatigue accumulating faster than recovering; early overtraining signal | De-load this week — before the power starts dropping |
| **Power below target, RPE on target** | Session feels like correct RPE but watts 5–8% low | Fatigue, dehydration, poor fuelling, life stress, or calibration drift | Shorten the session; investigate the cause; do not grind through at higher RPE |
| **Power high, RPE low** | Hitting 290W on a 280W target and it feels like RPE 5–6 | Fitness has moved | Hold two more sessions at the higher watts; if the relationship holds, retest FTP |
| **Race/group ride mismatches** | Power unexpectedly high or low, RPE out of step | Pacing error — either went too hard early or sat in too long | Post-ride review; use for pacing calibration next race |

The signal is in trends over 14–28 days, not in single sessions. A single bad day can mean almost anything.

---

### Minimum Viable Logging System

One integer RPE for the session as a whole, logged post-ride. For interval sessions, a second integer for the average RPE of the work intervals. That's it. Logging takes 10 seconds; the trend across weeks is what's valuable.

**Failure modes to avoid:**
- Reading too much into a single bad session — look at the 14–28 day rolling average
- Letting RPE override prescribed effort on every hard day — "felt tired so I skipped" becomes a habit that undermines training load

## 4. SPRINT INTERVAL TRAINING (SIT)

### What SIT Is

**Classic protocol:** 4–6 × 30 seconds all-out / 4 minutes easy spinning recovery. Total session time: 25–30 minutes including warm-up.

**Two non-negotiables:**
1. **Intensity is maximal** — 130–200% FTP; not "hard", all-out. The intensity is the entire mechanism.
2. **Full 4-minute recovery** — cutting rest turns SIT into a different, less effective session. The recovery allows each sprint to be genuinely maximal.

**SIT is not HIIT:**

| | SIT | HIIT |
|--|-----|------|
| Effort duration | 20–30 sec | 1–5 min |
| Intensity | All-out; well above VO2max | Hard; at/above VO2max (~105–115% FTP) |
| Recovery | 4 min (full) | 1–3 min (partial) |
| Primary adaptation | Peak power, neuromuscular, mitochondrial biogenesis signal | VO2max, threshold extension |

---

### Why SIT Works

- **Mitochondrial signalling:** All-out efforts trigger a strong AMPK/PGC-1α response — the signalling pathway that drives mitochondrial biogenesis. Signal per minute of work is significantly higher than steady-state riding.
- **VO2max stimulus:** Oxygen demand is pulled to or above VO2max within seconds and stays elevated during recovery as the body clears metabolic by-products. Cumulative time near VO2max can match a much longer standard VO2max interval session.
- **Type II fibre recruitment:** Maximal effort is the only reliable way to fully recruit fast-twitch fibres. SIT develops their aerobic capacity — critical for masters cyclists who lose type II fibres preferentially with age.
- **Lactate tolerance:** Repeated high-lactate / recovery cycles improve buffering capacity and lactate-as-fuel utilisation.

SIT fits the "hard 20%" of polarised training better than most session formats because it forces intensity to be unambiguously maximal.

### Session Execution

- **Sit down** — standing sprints recruit more upper body and produce less consistent power output
- **Build into each effort** — 3–5 sec acceleration, then hold highest sustainable power for remaining 25 sec
- **Target cadence:** 100–120 rpm; select a gear that reaches this range without spinning out
- **Take the full 4 min recovery** — even if you feel okay at 3 min
- **Mental commitment** — backing off in the last 10 seconds halves the stimulus

---

### Expected Adaptation Timeline

| Week | What to Expect |
|------|---------------|
| 1 | Brutal; significant soreness 2 days after |
| 3 | Soreness fades; sprint power on rep 1 starts climbing |
| 5 | Noticeable change on road — hard climbs have a different gear; sustained power feels easier |
| 7 | Peak SIT block; sprint power 10–15% above week 1 |
| 8–9 | Recovery week, then retest FTP — expect 5–15W jump in plateaued masters cyclists |

---

### When SIT Doesn't Work

- **Under-recovered athlete** — all-out efforts on a fatigued system accelerate the problem; fix recovery first
- **Iron-deficient or other physiological limiter** — get bloods before adding maximal training
- **Under-fuelled** — fasted maximal efforts produce nothing useful; ensure pre-ride carbohydrates
- **Thin aerobic base** — SIT requires a base to build on; do base first

## 5. FTP PLATEAU DIAGNOSIS

### The Five Causes

**Cause 1 — Grey-zone training (most common)**
Symptom: All sessions feel like a "good hard ride" but nothing is truly easy and nothing is truly hard; everything lives at RPE 6–7 (Z3).

Diagnostic: Pull the last 4 weeks of training files. What percentage of time sits above 70% of max HR but below lactate threshold? If >25%, this is the cause. In plateaued riders it's often 40%.

Fix: Cap easy rides at 70% max HR / RPE 4. Move 2 sessions/week to deliberate hard (threshold, VO2max). Let everything else sit clearly easy. Expect FTP movement in 6–8 weeks.

---

**Cause 2 — Chronic under-recovery**
Symptom: Training looks correct on paper; FTP still won't move; coincides with a change in life stress, sleep quality, or schedule.

Diagnostic: Has anything outside training changed in the last 6 months? Sleep duration, work stress, alcohol, travel? A plateau that coincided with a life change is almost always this.

Three sub-causes:
- Sleep < 7 hours, 3+ nights/week — growth hormone window for adaptation is concentrated in deep sleep
- Deload cadence too long — 3:1 cycle for most amateurs; 2:1 when life load is heavy
- Life-stress load — competes for the same cortisol bandwidth as training

Fix: Bring deload cadence to 2:1 for two cycles. Reduce volume 30–40%, protect sleep aggressively. Most riders see FTP move in the second cycle.

---

**Cause 3 — Base aerobic underdevelopment**
Symptom: Big numbers fresh; power drops sharply in hour 3–4 of a long ride; multiple years of interval-heavy training with no dedicated base phase.

Diagnostic: Is power at hour 4 of a 4-hour ride >10% lower than hour 1 at the same heart rate? Normal drift is 5–7%; >10% is base underdevelopment.

Fix: 12 weeks of Z2 base with one threshold session per week and no VO2max work. Most experienced amateurs resist this. Those who do it almost always come out with a higher absolute FTP and a far higher durable FTP.

---

**Cause 4 — Under-fuelling**
Symptom: Hard sessions feel hollow; recovery is poor despite sleep being fine; training load looks appropriate on paper.

Diagnostic: Are you consuming 60–90g carbohydrate/hr from the first hour of any ride over 90 minutes? Are you eating more on hard training days, not less?

Sub-causes:
- Daily energy availability below 30 kcal/kg LBM/day — crashes hormones and suppresses adaptation
- In-ride under-fuelling — body drops into glycogen debt before hunger signals; the recovery cost appears in the next session

Fix: Front-load daily fuelling around the workout. 1.0–1.5g carbs/kg in the 2 hours before a hard session. 60–90g/hr in-ride on sessions >90 min. 1.6–2.0g protein/kg/day distributed across meals. FTP often moves within 2 weeks of fixing this.

---

**Cause 5 — Measurement noise**
Symptom: FTP appears flat but test conditions have varied.

Diagnostic: Have you used the same protocol, same equipment, same conditions, same physical state (well-rested vs fatigued) for the last 3 FTP tests?

Sources of drift: trainer calibration, power meter zero-offset, different course/trainer, different fatigue state, room temperature, tyre pressure. Two or three of these together produce 3–5% drift — enough to mask real improvement or fake a plateau.

Fix: Standardise every variable. Same test, same trainer or loop, calibrated equipment, same time of day, well-rested, same warm-up. Most riders find that 1–2 of their "stalled" tests were fine and 1 "improvement" wasn't real.

---

### Diagnostic Order

1. **Measurement noise first** — if the test isn't reliable, none of the other interventions can be evaluated
2. **Intensity distribution** — single biggest lever
3. **Recovery** — check deload cadence and sleep
4. **Base** — check durability under fatigue
5. **Fuelling** — check daily energy and in-ride intake

Most plateaued riders have two causes simultaneously. Grey-zone + under-recovery cluster together; base underdevelopment + under-fuelling cluster together.

## 6. HEAT TRAINING

### Mechanism and Performance Effect

Heat exposure triggers a two-phase response:
1. **Days 1–3:** Immediate plasma volume expansion — improves cardiovascular stability and oxygen delivery alone
2. **Day 4 onward:** New red blood cell production (same hemoglobin mass response targeted by altitude and EPO)

**Stabilised effects after ~5 weeks (3 sessions/week):**

| Adaptation | Magnitude |
|-----------|-----------|
| Hemoglobin mass increase | 3–4% |
| VO2max improvement | ~6% |
| FTP gain (trained amateurs) | 20–30W |
| HR reduction at same power | 5–8 bpm |
| Improved sweat response | Earlier onset, reduced sodium loss |

Hemoglobin effect is comparable to 3 weeks at altitude (2,100m). Heat training is more universally responsive than altitude — genetic non-responders to altitude still respond to heat.

**Post-altitude retention (Ronnestad 2024):** Elite cyclists who added heat sessions after a 3-week altitude camp retained hemoglobin gains for 3.5 weeks. The control group lost ~71% of gains over the same period.

---

### Home Protocol

**Equipment:** Indoor trainer, smallest available room, space heater (target ambient 25–30°C), winter kit (long-sleeve, tights, hat, gloves), no fan, core temperature sensor (or in-ear thermometer between intervals), scale (pre/post session weight), electrolyte drink.

**Session structure:**

| Phase | Duration | Intensity | Notes |
|-------|---------|-----------|-------|
| Warm-up | 5–10 min | Easy Z1 | Winter kit on; begin building heat |
| Heat ramp | ~20 min | Z2–Z3 | Get core temp to 38.5°C |
| Main work | 30–40 min | Z1 bottom of Z2 | Hold 38.5°C; intensity drops as adaptation develops |
| Cool-down | 10–15 min | Layers off, windows open | Cold fluids now acceptable |

**Target core temperature:** 38.5°C. **Hard stop:** 39°C or any dizziness, nausea, headache, or cessation of sweating.

**Frequency:** 3 sessions/week with at least 1 easy day between each. Duration: 2–6 weeks (2 weeks for front-end response; 4–6 weeks for full hemoglobin stabilisation).

**Hydration:** ≥1L room-temperature electrolyte fluid/hr. Cold fluids blunt the adaptive response — avoid in-session. Replace 150% of body weight lost over the following hours. If body weight drop exceeds 2% in a single session, the protocol is too aggressive.

---

### Where It Fits in a Training Plan

Heat training works best as a layered intervention on a strong aerobic base, inside a deliberate build phase, with a target event that justifies the discomfort. Most effective 3–4 weeks before a hot-weather event (combined thermoregulation pre-adaptation + hemoglobin gains).

Without a hot target event: the protocol still raises hemoglobin and FTP, but the discomfort cost is real. Compare against the value of a high-volume Z2 block or a polarised threshold block in the same window.

## 7. HILL REPEATS

### Session Structures

**Hill selection:** 5–8% gradient; 3–20 min in length (match to session goal); clean tarmac, low traffic; rideable recovery descent.

| Session Type | Interval | Intensity | Recovery | Reps | Cadence | Goal |
|-------------|---------|-----------|----------|------|---------|------|
| Threshold | 5–8 min | 95–105% FTP | Descend easy 3–5 min | 4–6 | 75–85 rpm | FTP development |
| VO2max | 3–4 min | 106–120% FTP | Descend easy 3–4 min | 5–8 | 80–90 rpm | Peak aerobic power |
| Strength-endurance | 6–10 min | 85–95% FTP | Descend easy 5–6 min | 3–5 | 60–70 rpm seated | Muscular endurance, low-cadence torque |
| Short power | 60–90 sec | 130–150% FTP | Descend easy 2–3 min | 8–12 | 85–95 rpm | Anaerobic punch over the top |

---

### Pacing Rules

- **Pace rep 1 at the bottom of the target zone.** Rep 1 should feel controlled. The last rep should hurt.
- **10% rule:** If power on the final rep drops more than 10% below rep 1, the session is done. Junk intervals at 80% of target build fatigue without adaptation.
- Gravity enforces honest effort — no coasting, no hiding.

---

### Progression (6–8 Week Block)

| Week | Volume | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| 1–2 | 4 reps × 4 min | Establish the pattern |
| 3–4 | 5 reps × 5 min | Add rep first |
| 5–6 | 6 reps × 5 min | Then add volume |
| 7–8 | 6 reps × 6 min | Then add duration |

Volume before duration before intensity. Flipping this order accumulates fatigue without adaptation.

## 8. POWER-TO-WEIGHT RATIO (W/KG)

### W/kg Benchmarks

| W/kg | Level | Context |
|------|-------|---------|
| 1.5–2.5 | Recreational | Comfortable on flat terrain |
| 2.5–3.0 | Fitness cyclist | Completes sportives; comfortable in groups |
| 3.0–3.5 | Competitive amateur | Holds own in club races; strong on climbs |
| 3.5–4.0 | Strong amateur | Competitive Cat 3–4; strong sportive performer |
| 4.0–4.5 | Elite amateur | Competitive at national level; top 10% |
| 4.5–5.0 | Semi-pro | Domestique-level professional |
| 5.0–6.0 | Professional | World Tour level |
| 6.0+ | Elite professional | Grand Tour contender |

W/kg is the dominant factor on any sustained climb over 5 minutes. On flat courses, absolute power and aerodynamics dominate.

**Calculation:** W/kg = FTP ÷ body weight (kg)

---

### Two Levers: Power Up or Weight Down?

| Focus on **Power** when: | Focus on **Weight** when: |
|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Body fat already below 12% (men) or 18% (women) | Body fat above 15% (men) or 22% (women) |
| First 2–3 years of structured training | Training consistently 3+ years and power is plateauing |
| Losing power when dieting | Weight loss achievable through better nutrition, not restriction |

For most amateurs at 3.0–4.0 W/kg, the fastest path to improvement is body composition — not through restriction but through fuelling structure. A 6–8kg weight reduction while maintaining FTP can produce ~0.3–0.5 W/kg gain in 12 weeks. An equivalent gain through power alone could take 6–12 months.

## 9. BODY COMPOSITION

### Why Lean Mass Matters More Than Scale Weight

Two riders at 75kg with different compositions produce very different power outputs:
- Rider A: 12% body fat → 66kg lean mass
- Rider B: 20% body fat → 60kg lean mass
- Same scale weight; Rider A has ~6kg more muscle producing power

Scale weight is one number. Body composition gives two: how much is engine, how much is cargo.

---

### Target Ranges for Cyclists

| Level | Men (Body Fat %) | Women (Body Fat %) |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Recreational | 15–25% | 20–30% |
| Competitive amateur | 10–15% | 16–22% |
| Elite amateur | 8–12% | 14–18% |
| Professional | 6–10% | 12–16% |

Going below 8% (men) or 14% (women) carries real health risks and should only happen under professional supervision.

---

### Measurement Methods

| Method | Accuracy | Practical Use |
|--------|---------|--------------|
| Smart scale (Withings, Garmin) — bioelectrical impedance | Low absolute accuracy; consistent relative | Track weekly trends under same conditions (morning, fasted, post-bathroom) |
| DEXA scan | High absolute accuracy | Once to calibrate; use smart scale for ongoing tracking |
| Skinfold callipers | Accurate with trained tester | Same tester every time; variable otherwise |

The smart scale's value is consistency for trend tracking, not absolute accuracy.

---

### Body Composition Approach

- High carbohydrate intake around hard training sessions; reduced carbohydrate on easy days and rest days
- Weekly caloric deficit accumulates naturally without restricting food when the body needs it
- Never under-fuel a hard session — the deficit comes from easy days, not training days
- Preserves power, maintains recovery, avoids binge-restrict cycle

This approach (reducing carbs on easy days / rest days, not on quality days) is how W/kg improves without losing FTP.
