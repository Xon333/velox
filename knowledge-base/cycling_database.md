# CYCLING DATABASE — GENERAL PRINCIPLES

---

## 1. TRAINING

### What Is FTP
Functional Threshold Power (FTP) is the highest average power sustainable for approximately one hour. It sits at or near lactate threshold — the point where lactate production exceeds clearance. FTP anchors all training zones and directly determines climbing speed (W/kg). Every zone is a percentage of FTP; an inaccurate FTP means every session is misprescribed.

---

### FTP Testing Methods

| Method | Protocol | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| 20-minute test | Max sustainable effort × 0.95 | Most widely used; warm up 15–20 min with short efforts |
| Ramp test | Power increases 1 step/min until failure | Less mentally demanding; may overestimate FTP in some athletes |
| 60-minute test | Full hour at max sustainable effort | Gold standard; impractical for most non-racers |
| AI detection | Platform estimates from workout data | Accurate, no disruption to training rhythm |

Test every 6–8 weeks. More frequent testing disrupts training and adds unnecessary fatigue.

---

### Training Zones (7-Zone Model)

| Zone | Name | % FTP | Duration / Feel |
|------|------|-------|-----------------|
| Z1 | Active Recovery | < 55% | Very easy; no fatigue accumulation |
| Z2 | Endurance | 56–75% | Conversation pace; aerobic base |
| Z3 | Tempo | 76–90% | Moderately hard; the grey zone — avoid overusing |
| Z4 | Threshold | 91–105% | Hard but sustainable; FTP-specific stimulus |
| Z5 | VO2max | 106–120% | Very hard; 3–8 min efforts |
| Z6 | Anaerobic | 121–150% | Short maximal efforts; < 2 min |
| Z7 | Neuromuscular | > 150% | Sprints; full neuromuscular power |

The most common amateur error is spending excessive time in Z3. Z3 feels productive but is too hard to be truly restorative and too easy to drive meaningful adaptation. Make easy rides genuinely easy and hard rides genuinely hard.

---

### The Three Training Pillars

**Pillar 1 — Aerobic Base (Zone 2)**
The aerobic base is the foundation FTP sits on. Without it, interval work plateaus by week 6–8. Zone 2 builds mitochondrial density, capillary networks, and fat oxidation capacity. Approximately 80% of total training time should be in Z1–Z2. The Saturday long ride (3–4 hours) is the single highest-leverage session for base building.

**Pillar 2 — VO2max Development**
FTP is capped by VO2max. Without raising the ceiling periodically, threshold work alone will plateau. VO2max intervals (3–5 min at 106–120% FTP) push the ceiling higher. The classic protocol: 4×4 min at VO2max power, 4 min recovery. Cycle VO2max blocks with threshold blocks rather than running both simultaneously.

**Pillar 3 — Threshold Work**
The direct stimulus for FTP improvement. Sustained efforts at 91–105% FTP train the body to produce power at lactate threshold. Variety prevents accommodation — do not repeat the same session every week.

---

### Key Threshold Sessions

| Session | Protocol | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| 2×20 | 2 × 20 min at 95–100% FTP / 10 min recovery | Bread and butter; most common |
| 3×15 | 3 × 15 min at 95–100% FTP / 8 min recovery | Slightly lower fatigue than 2×20 |
| 4×10 | 4 × 10 min at 100–105% FTP / 5 min recovery | Higher intensity, shorter blocks |
| Over-unders | 20 min alternating 1 min at 110% / 2 min at 95% FTP | Teaches lactate clearance at threshold |
| Sweet spot | 2×30 min at 88–93% FTP / 10 min recovery | High stimulus, manageable fatigue; good for base-to-build transition |

---

### Weekly Structure (8–10 hrs/week)

| Day | Session | Duration |
|-----|---------|----------|
| Monday | Full rest | — |
| Tuesday | Quality 1 — Threshold or VO2max | 60–90 min |
| Wednesday | Easy Z2 | 60–90 min |
| Thursday | Quality 2 — Threshold or sweet spot | 75–90 min |
| Friday | Rest or easy optional spin | 45–60 min |
| Saturday | Long ride — Z2 | 3–4 hrs |
| Sunday | Easy Z2 | 60–90 min |

Two quality sessions per week is the optimal dose for most amateurs. More compromises recovery. The Saturday long ride is protected — it is the most important single session of the week.

---

### Annual Periodisation Framework

| Phase | Duration | Primary Focus | Intensity Split |
|-------|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Base | 10–16 weeks | Aerobic volume, Z2 | ~90% easy, ~10% moderate |
| Build | 8–12 weeks | Threshold + VO2max intervals | ~80% easy, ~20% hard |
| Peak | 4–6 weeks | Race-specific sharpening | ~75% easy, ~25% hard |
| Taper/Event | 1–2 weeks | Reduced volume, freshness | Low volume |
| Transition | 2–4 weeks | Rest and mental reset | Unstructured, low load |

The base phase is non-negotiable. Skipping it to begin intervals early causes plateaus by week 6. Aerobic capacity built in base is the ceiling for every later phase. Include a recovery week (30–50% volume reduction) every 3–4 weeks throughout all phases.

---

### Common Training Mistakes

- **All threshold, no variety** — the body accommodates to repeated identical sessions. Rotate threshold, VO2max, sweet spot, and over-unders.
- **Easy rides too hard** — Z3 creep on recovery days compromises quality sessions. Check power files.
- **Skipping the base phase** — intervals without base plateau quickly and don't transfer to long-ride durability.
- **No recovery week** — skipping the planned 30–50% volume drop every 3–4 weeks is the most common cause of stagnation.
- **Testing too frequently** — disrupts training rhythm. Every 6–8 weeks is sufficient.
- **Under-fuelling hard sessions** — produces lower power, less adaptation, worse recovery. Fuel quality sessions.

## 2. NUTRITION

### Fuelling Philosophy
Modern cycling nutrition is based on fuelling for the work required — eating to support training stress, not enforcing an arbitrary daily deficit. Energy availability (calories in minus exercise calories, divided by lean body mass) should stay at or above 30 kcal/kg LBM/day. Below 30 kcal/kg LBM/day: hormonal function, recovery capacity, and bone density are progressively compromised.

---

### Daily Macronutrient Targets (8–12 hrs/week training)

| Day Type | Carbohydrate | Protein | Fat |
|----------|-------------|---------|-----|
| Rest day | 3–5 g/kg | 1.6–2.0 g/kg | Fill remaining calories |
| Easy training day | 5–7 g/kg | 1.6–2.0 g/kg | Fill remaining calories |
| Hard training day | 7–10 g/kg | 1.8–2.2 g/kg | Fill remaining calories |

Carbohydrates are reduced on rest days and light days, never on quality training days. Protein is held consistent throughout. Fat fills the remainder once carb and protein targets are met.

---

### In-Ride Nutrition

| Ride Type | Duration | Carbs/hr | Strategy |
|-----------|----------|----------|----------|
| Easy Z2 | < 90 min | 0–30 g | Water usually sufficient |
| Easy Z2 | > 90 min | 45–60 g | Begin fuelling at ~45 min |
| Threshold / VO2max | Any | 60–90 g | Fuel before and throughout |
| Racing / hard multi-hour | Any | 90–120 g | Glucose:fructose 2:1 mix required above 60 g/hr |

Glucose absorption is capped at ~60 g/hr when using a single transporter. Above this, a 2:1 glucose:fructose mix is required to engage multiple gut transporters. Gut adaptation takes 4–6 weeks of progressive training — start at 60 g/hr, add 10 g every 2 weeks.

---

### Race Weight
A 2–4% body weight reduction without power loss is realistic. More risks performance and health.
- Achieve weight loss in the off-season, not during race build-up
- Small daily deficit of 200–400 kcal over 8–12 weeks
- Never cut carbohydrates on quality training days
- Cut on rest days only
- Maintain strength training through any weight loss phase to protect lean mass

---

### Hydration

| Scenario | Fluid | Sodium |
|----------|-------|--------|
| Daily baseline | Drink to thirst; urine pale yellow | Covered by normal diet |
| In-ride | 500–1000 ml/hr depending on heat and effort | 500–1000 mg/L when sweating heavily or riding >90 min |
| Post-ride rehydration | 125–150% of body weight lost over 4–6 hrs | Include sodium with fluid |

Over-drinking (hyponatraemia) is more common in amateur ultra-endurance events than dehydration. Match fluid to need.

---

### Recovery Nutrition
See Recovery section for full protocol. Core rule: 1.0–1.2 g/kg carbohydrate plus 20–40 g protein within 30 minutes of finishing any hard or long session.

---

### Common Nutrition Mistakes

- **Under-fuelling hard sessions** — 60 g/hr minimum on any session over 75 minutes; more on intervals.
- **Treating every ride identically** — recovery rides need very little fuel; long and hard rides need a lot. Match food to work.
- **Restricting in race week** — race-week intake should top up glycogen, not strip body fat.
- **Supplement overreliance** — evidence-backed list is short: caffeine (pre-quality session), sodium (long rides), creatine (year-round), vitamin D (winter check). Everything else is marketing.

## 3. RECOVERY

### Core Principle
Recovery is the mechanism by which training produces adaptation — not the absence of training. The four non-negotiable levers: sleep, post-session fuelling, genuinely easy days, and a recovery week every 3–4 weeks. Addressing these four eliminates the need for almost every recovery supplement.

---

### Sleep

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Duration | 7–9 hours |
| Wake time | Consistent daily — including weekends |
| Room temperature | 16–19°C |
| Pre-bed routine | No screens 60 min before; no hard training within 3 hrs |
| Optional supplement | Magnesium glycinate 400 mg, 60 min before bed |

Sleep deprivation effects are rapid: a single bad night reduces glycogen storage, increases RPE at fixed power, and impairs immune function. Consistent sleep under 7 hours produces measurable time-to-exhaustion losses within 5–7 days. Sleep is the highest-return recovery input available.

---

### Recovery Nutrition (0–4 Hour Window)

| Window | Target Intake |
|--------|--------------|
| 0–30 min | 1.0–1.2 g/kg carbohydrate + 20–40 g protein; liquid acceptable if appetite suppressed |
| 30–120 min | Balanced meal — carbohydrate, protein, vegetables |
| 2–4 hrs | Second carbohydrate-rich meal; essential before back-to-back hard days |

Prioritise the 0–30 min window after any session over 90 minutes or any quality session regardless of duration.

---

### Active Recovery vs Full Rest

| Scenario | Active Recovery (< 60% FTP) | Full Rest |
|----------|----------------------------|-----------|
| Day after moderate session | ✓ | |
| Day after very hard / long session | | ✓ |
| Minor general fatigue | ✓ | |
| Illness or RHR elevated > 5 bpm | | ✓ |
| HRV trending down 3+ consecutive days | | ✓ |
| Back-to-back hard days ahead | ✓ | |

Active recovery must be genuinely easy — under 60% FTP throughout, conversation easy. Any power spikes into Z3 mean the ride is training, not recovery.

---

### Recovery Week

Every 3–4 weeks of structured training: reduce volume by 30–50% and drop intensity slightly. This is when training converts to fitness — skipping it is the most common path to plateau.

**Signs a recovery week is needed early:**
- Resting HR elevated 5+ bpm for 3 consecutive mornings
- HRV trending downward despite normal sleep
- Quality session power dropping at same RPE
- Sleep onset taking longer than usual
- Flat mood, absent motivation

---

### Recovery Tracking

| Metric | What to Track | Action Threshold |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| HRV | 7-day rolling average | Downward trend 3+ days → reduce training load |
| Resting heart rate | Morning before rising | > 5 bpm above baseline → consider rest day |
| Sleep duration | Nightly total | < 7 hrs consistently → address before modifying training |
| Perceived recovery | 1–10 scale (morning) | < 5 on multiple consecutive mornings → de-load |
| Morning body weight | Fasted, daily | Drop > 1% per week → increase caloric intake |

Use data for patterns, not individual readings. A single low HRV reading rarely justifies changing a session. A 7-day downward trend across multiple metrics does.

---

### Common Recovery Problems

| Problem | Most Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|------------------|-----|
| Tired all week despite easy days | Easy days not actually easy | Check power files for Z3 creep |
| Poor sleep after evening hard sessions | Late training + sympathetic nervous system activation | Move session earlier; fuel and hydrate immediately post-session |
| Repeated illness in winter | Under-fuelling + sleep deficit | Check vitamin D; fuel hard sessions; achieve 8+ hrs sleep |
| Can't hit previous intensity after a break | Returning too quickly | 3 weeks of Z2 base before reintroducing intervals |

## 4. STRENGTH TRAINING

### Why Cyclists Should Lift

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Power output | Higher 1RM squat correlates with higher 5-second peak power |
| Durability | Stronger connective tissue absorbs load that otherwise becomes injury |
| Cycling economy | 1–3% efficiency gain at submaximal intensities from heavy compound work |
| Body composition | Protects lean mass during fat-loss phases |
| Bone density | Cycling alone does not load bone; resistance training is required |

Heavy strength training (4–6 reps, compound lifts) is appropriate year-round, not only in the off-season. The goal is force production, not hypertrophy. Endurance training suppresses hypertrophy — cyclists who lift heavy gain force without significant mass gain.

---

### Core Programme — Heavy Compound Lifts

| Exercise | Sets | Reps | Primary Benefit |
|----------|------|------|-----------------|
| Back squat (or goblet squat) | 4 | 4–6 | Lower body force production |
| Romanian deadlift | 4 | 4–6 | Posterior chain, hamstrings, glutes |
| Bulgarian split squat | 3 | 5 each leg | Single-leg stability, hip balance |
| Nordic hamstring curl | 3 | 5–6 | Hamstring injury prevention |
| Hip thrust | 3 | 6–8 | Glute activation and power |
| Bent-over row | 3 | 6–8 | Upper back, posture on the bike |
| Overhead press | 3 | 6–8 | Shoulder stability, sprint position |

Two sessions per week of this list year-round is the highest-return strength programme available for most amateurs. Avoid machine-based substitutes (leg press, etc.) — they do not replicate the neural demand of compound free-weight movements.

---

### Weekly Template (8–12 hrs Riding + Gym)

| Day | Bike Session | Gym Session | Notes |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Monday | Rest | — | Full rest |
| Tuesday | Quality 1 (75–90 min) | — | Threshold or VO2max |
| Wednesday | Easy Z2 (60–90 min) | Strength | Minimum 6 hr gap between ride and lift |
| Thursday | Quality 2 (75–90 min) | — | Threshold or sweet spot |
| Friday | Rest or easy spin | Strength (optional) | If lifting, moderate loads only |
| Saturday | Long ride (3–4 hrs) | — | Most important session — protected |
| Sunday | Easy Z2 (60–90 min) | — | Flush legs |

**In-season adjustment:** Drop to 1 strength session/week. Keep rep range at 4–6 (maintain intensity), reduce sets from 4 to 2–3. Never drop to zero.

---

### Masters Cyclists (40+)

After 40, sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) accelerates and strength training becomes non-negotiable for maintaining power and durability.

- Heavy compound lifting 2×/week year-round with full recovery between sessions
- Add low-volume plyometric jumps 1×/week to preserve fast-twitch fibres
- Protein intake at 1.8–2.2 g/kg to support recovery from lifting
- Allow longer recovery between hard days than at younger ages — programme accordingly

---

### Mobility (Minimum Effective Dose)

| When | What | Duration |
|------|------|----------|
| Before each lift | Hip and thoracic mobility | 5–10 min |
| Daily | Hips, ankles, thoracic spine, lats | 10 min |
| Optional | Yoga or Pilates | 1×/week |

The desk-bound cyclist's primary restrictions are hip flexors and thoracic mobility. Addressing both resolves most lower back pain complaints on the bike.

---

### Common Strength Training Mistakes

- **Stopping in-season** — adaptations are lost by July if lifting stops in March. Maintain 1 session/week minimum.
- **Too many reps, too light** — 3×12 at light weight is hypertrophy, not strength. Cyclists need force production: 4–6 reps at genuinely challenging load.
- **Skipping upper body** — stronger arms and back maintain position, sprint harder, and protect in crashes.
- **Lifting the day before key bike sessions** — always lift after easy bike days, never the day before threshold or VO2max work.
- **Confusing soreness with progress** — 4-day DOMS means the programme is overdosed. Reduce volume.
